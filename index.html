§ 01 · The Thesis

I very frequently get the question: "What's going to change in the next ten years?" I almost never get the question: "What's not going to change in the next ten years?" And I submit to you that that second question is actually the more important of the two — because you can build a business strategy around the things that are stable in time.

Jeff Bezos, 2012

Trend forecasting is a two-billion-dollar industry dedicated to naming the new. Most of it arrives eighteen months late.

By the time a trend has been identified, named, and written up in a $40,000 report, it is already metabolizing into the culture's bloodstream. The client is six months late when the report is delivered and up to two years late by the time anything actually ships. The waste is staggering — and the problem is not that the forecasters are bad at their jobs. The problem is that they're answering the wrong question.

This report does the opposite. Instead of asking what's new, it asks what's old — what human needs have been driving behavior so reliably that every major trend of the last decade can be traced back to one of them.

We call them substrates: the bedrock beneath the surface. Trends are the weather. Substrates are the climate. If you understand the climate, you can predict the weather. If you only read weather reports, you will always be surprised.

01

Connection

How we find each other when the old containers break.

The enduring need for belonging, intimacy, and shared presence — for being witnessed by another human being in a way a screen cannot replicate. It is the bedrock need of a primate species that evolved in bands of forty.

What broke
Third spaces & alcohol-based networking
What emerged
Run clubs, sweatworking, sober bars, book clubs

American "third spaces" — the cafés, bars, churches, bowling leagues, and community centers that formed the middle layer between home and work — have been eroding since Ray Oldenburg first named them in 1989. Car-centric zoning ate into walkable neighborhoods. Big-box retail replaced the corner store. The 2008 recession took out the rest. Then, just as the decade we studied began, alcohol — the social lubricant of the old container — entered the steepest decline since Prohibition. Gen Z drinks roughly thirty percent less than millennials did at the same age.

The need did not go anywhere. It simply rerouted. Between 2016 and 2026, run clubs went from niche to standard-issue urban infrastructure. Pickleball became the fastest-growing sport in America. Sober bars opened in every major city. Sweatworking — networking over a workout class instead of a whisky — is now the default in industries that used to run on steak-house expense accounts. Even the return of the dinner party, reported in earnest in The New York Times in early 2026, is the same ancient primate impulse dressed up in this season's tablecloth.

The opposite of addiction is not sobriety. It is human connection.

— Johann Hari

02

Coping

Nervous-system infrastructure for an overwhelming century.

The enduring need to metabolize uncertainty, anxiety, and overwhelm — to find a reliable way to calm a nervous system that is constantly being asked to process more information, more threat, and more change than it evolved to handle.

What broke
Religious ritual · steady employment · the assumption tomorrow looks like today
What emerged
Meditation apps, weighted blankets, sourdough, cozy gaming, ketamine therapy

The old containers for processing difficulty — confessional booths, grief circles, church basements, the village elder — were not replaced when they emptied out. They were simply abandoned. For a while, in the late twentieth century, Americans pretended that therapy and a steady job would suffice. The decade we studied is what happened when it turned out they didn't.

Headspace and Calm broke a billion downloads. Weighted blankets became a $600 million category out of nothing. Sourdough starters became pandemic fixtures not because Americans suddenly discovered wild yeast, but because the gentle, repetitive, controllable ritual of feeding something alive was the cheapest available antidote to the feeling that nothing else in the world was under control. Stanley cups became hydration rituals. Cozy gaming — Stardew Valley, Animal Crossing, Cozy Grove — turned video games from stress into solace. Ketamine therapy came out of the closet. Bed rotting was named, romanticized, and defended in print. By 2026, "romanticizing your life" was the most popular self-help genre on TikTok.

None of this is soft. It is load-bearing. It is how an entire generation is keeping its nervous system online.

Almost everything will work again if you unplug it for a few minutes — including you.

— Anne Lamott

03

Agency

Authorship over a life the systems can't be trusted with.

The enduring need to feel authorship over one's own life — to act on the world rather than be acted upon by it. Especially when large systems (employers, platforms, food supply chains, medical establishments) feel rigged, indifferent, or quietly hostile.

What broke
Trust in institutions · the post-war social contract · faith in the industrial food system
What emerged
Etsy · Substack · Great Resignation · raw milk · seed oil discourse · homesteading

Somewhere between 2016 and 2026, a critical mass of Americans stopped believing that the systems they were part of were operating in their interest. It was not one event. It was the slow accumulation of losing faith in employers (Great Recession, 2008), news (Facebook algorithm era, 2016), medical consensus (opioid crisis, 2017; vaccine discourse, 2021), information itself (GPT-3, 2020; GPT-4, 2023), and finally, in the closing years, food — as seed oil discourse and raw milk movements moved from the margins to the mainstream.

The response was not resignation. It was a two-decade-long sprint toward personal authorship. Etsy turned "I made this myself" into a $12 billion economy. Substack pulled writers out of institutional publishing. The Great Resignation pulled workers out of institutional employment. Sourdough starters and Victory gardens gave locked-down families the illusion — and occasionally the reality — of feeding themselves. By the middle of the decade, "build in public" had become a brand posture, homesteading was a content genre, and RFK's campaign was running on the conviction that the FDA could not be trusted to keep food safe. These are not unrelated movements. They are the same substrate expressing itself through whatever container each tribe could grab.

I would rather be ruined than changed.

— W. H. Auden

04

Vitality

The body as the thing that has to still work.

The enduring need to feel embodied, energized, and alive in a body that works — to not be sick, not be tired, not be ambiently unwell. It is the most bedrock of the five, and the most reliable to commodify.

What broke
Faith in the medical establishment · industrial food system · "exercise is enough"
What emerged
CrossFit · Whoop · cold plunge · beef tallow · Ozempic · longevity clinics

The decade we studied opened with CrossFit at its cultural peak and closed with Ozempic on the cover of The Atlantic. In between, the American relationship with the body underwent two enormous shifts in quick succession. The first was the realization, accelerated by Peloton and Oura and Whoop, that the body was measurable — that sleep, HRV, recovery, and strain could be instrumented and optimized like a server. The second, more recent, was the realization that willpower had been a lie the whole time — that metabolism was chemistry, that weight was not a moral failing, and that a weekly injection could resolve a problem decades of jogging and kale had not.

Underneath all of it: the enduring, pre-rational need to feel alive in your own body. CrossFit, keto, Whole30, Bulletproof coffee, intermittent fasting, Zone 2 cardio, cold plunge, sauna, mouth taping, HRV charting, beef tallow skincare, raw milk, regenerative meat, longevity clinics — every one of them is a different tactic on the same substrate. The question is never is vitality real. The question is which container the market will put it in next.

You are allowed to live in a body that works. Everything else can be rearranged around that.

— found on a gym chalkboard, 2024

05

Status

Becoming legible to your tribe without having to explain yourself.

The enduring need to be legible to one's tribe — to signal, without having to explain, who you are, what you value, and whose approval you care about. Status is the oldest enduring need in marketing, and it never stops adapting.

What broke
Mass-media as universal signaling layer · the middle class as stable identity container
What emerged
Quiet luxury · Labubu · Sprinter vans · Stanley cups · dumbphones as reverse-status

Status used to be simple: there was television, there were department stores, and there were logos that everyone could read. If you were rich, you wore Ralph Lauren and everyone knew it. If you were counter-cultural, you wore Carhartt and everyone knew that too. The shared mass-media playing field made status legible across class and subculture at once.

That shared field is gone. In its place, a thousand micro-tribes each developed their own dialect of signal. Quiet luxury said: if you have to ask, I'm not trying to talk to you. Crypto PFPs said: I was early. Labubu collections said: I play, and I can afford to play. Sprinter vans said: I have more freedom than you. Stanley cups said: I am a functional adult woman with priorities. Dumbphones said: I am secure enough not to need the firehose. Even Carhartt was reclaimed at double the price. Every one of these is the same ancient primate impulse — let my tribe see who I am without my having to say so — rewired for the age of fragmented media.

Aroney's favorite line, from Karl Lagerfeld, captures the motion: trendy is the last stage before tacky. The substrate is always the same. It is the market that keeps inventing new clothes for it.

Trendy is the last stage before tacky.

— Karl Lagerfeld

§ 04 · The Method

Ask What Broke.

Traditional trend reports describe the surface. They name the new aesthetic, the new ritual, the new vocabulary — and by naming them, they freeze something that is already in motion. We take a different approach.

For every trend we encounter, we ask three questions in this exact order:

  1. ①
    What broke?

    What historic container for this need has degraded, disappeared, or lost legitimacy? If no container broke, the trend is probably a fashion cycle, not a substrate expression.

  2. ②
    What enduring need got rerouted?

    Which of the five substrates is carrying the demand the broken container used to serve? Trends usually map to a primary substrate and one or two secondary co-drivers.

  3. ③
    What new container is carrying it?

    What's the new vessel — and is it actually serving the need, or just signaling the problem? (The difference matters. Cottagecore often signals the need for Agency without actually delivering any.)

Only after those three questions do we ask what the trend looks like. Aesthetic comes last. It has to, because aesthetic is the only thing the market can sell you — and the only thing the forecasting industry can name.
